About

Memorax is a simple but powerful daily review tracker based on cognitive psychology. It is not for storing the content itself — it only saves the location (chapter names, page numbers, practice sets) and then tells you exactly when to revise. You can also open Previous Records to see what you logged on any past day from the last 60 days, so you always know what to revisit and where to find it.

What to write: The location of what you studied — for example, "Maths Ch.4" or "English Notebook pages 48–53".
What not to write: Things you do only once and don’t plan to revisit, like “writing class notes.”

How it works

  1. Log once per day. Add one quick line about what you studied. If you did nothing that day, write "nothing". If you miss a day, Memorax fills it as “not logged by user” so your days stay in order — and you can still modify it later in Previous Records (kept for 60 days).
  2. Smart timing. After you first learn something, Memorax brings it back at four key times: 1, 3, 7, and 14 days later. If something still feels shaky after the 14-day cycle, you can always review it again later — for example after 15 days, 30 days, then 3 months — to lock it in for the long term.
  3. One clear list. Each day shows exactly what to review — newest items first — plus any missed reviews, so nothing slips through the cracks. If you ever forget what an entry refers to, open Previous Records to see the exact chapter or pages you logged.
  4. Quick finish. When you’re done, mark the whole day’s review as “Revised.” One click. No extra steps.

Why it’s effective

  • Works with your brain, not against it. Memorax is grounded in cognitive psychology and the way memory strengthens with well-timed reviews.
  • Spacing effect. We forget fast right after learning. Short, timed reviews (1, 3, 7, 14 days) reinforce memory before it fades.
  • Retrieval practice. Actively recalling from memory beats passively re-reading — it makes learning stick.
  • “Just hard enough.” Reviewing after a little delay feels slightly effortful — the good kind of struggle that boosts retention (“desirable difficulty”).
  • Fewer decisions, stronger habit. One daily list, one quick log, one click to finish — less friction means more consistency and better memory.
